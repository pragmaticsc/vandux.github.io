Astrophysics explores the physical properties and dynamic processes of celestial objects, from stars to galaxies. It combines principles of physics with astronomical observations to understand phenomena like black holes and the cosmic microwave background.

The study of stellar evolution reveals how stars form, live, and die. Massive stars may end in spectacular supernovae, leaving behind neutron stars or black holes, while smaller stars like our Sun become white dwarfs.

Cosmology, a branch of astrophysics, investigates the universe’s origin, structure, and fate. The Big Bang theory suggests the universe began 13.8 billion years ago, supported by evidence like the expansion observed by Hubble Space Telescope.

Dark matter, an invisible substance, influences gravitational effects in galaxies. Though it doesn’t emit or absorb light, its presence is inferred from galactic rotation curves and gravitational lensing, as studied in projects like the Dark Energy Survey.

Dark energy, another mysterious component, drives the accelerated expansion of the universe. Discovered through observations of distant supernovae, it constitutes roughly 68% of the universe’s energy, according to Planck mission data.

Exoplanet research has surged with missions like Kepler, identifying thousands of planets orbiting other stars. Astrophysicists analyze their atmospheres for signs of habitability, using techniques like transit spectroscopy.

Neutron stars, remnants of massive stars, are incredibly dense, with a teaspoon of their material weighing as much as a mountain. Their study, including pulsars, provides insights into extreme states of matter and gravitational waves.

The search for extraterrestrial intelligence, or SETI, uses radio telescopes to detect signals from advanced civilizations. Projects like SETI@home engage the public in analyzing data, pushing the boundaries of astrophysical exploration.
